It’s not unusual for students in the hard sciences to spend a lot of time in the lab, but Chemical Engineering major Taylor Little gets to work out “in the field” in ways many undergraduate students in her field don’t—as part of a community-based social justice research project.

Taylor, who is also an MLK scholar, worked with Dr. Daniela Terson de Paleville on the Minds In Motion project, which assesses how a physical fitness program during school hours may affect academic, physical, and social development in students at Hawthorne Elementary. Taylor assisted with pre- and post- testing with the students, recording/analyzing data, presenting the research at academic conferences, and expanding the project to an Indianapolis middle school, among other projects.

It’s not exactly the same kind of work as her major, but Taylor values the opportunity to work with more people and on a social justice issue—access to quality physical education—that matters to her.

“It’s nice to have more interactive research than just sitting in the lab and staring at stuff,” she observed. “I also think it made me more aware of issues with technology and how kids’ childhoods are changing [… and] the value of more traditional play.”

Taylor noted that working on this project has helped her develop more diverse academic and professional skills, such as presentation skills and thinking about how academic research can benefit the community.

“A huge part of engineering is thinking about how it affects other people, economics, and broader stuff like that,” she said. In the Minds and Motion project, researchers were concerned with “more than the main point of the project itself. How can it be replicated? How is it going to affect other populations?”

The Minds in Motion project is just one of the ways Taylor stays active in social justice concerns. As the former environmental ownership chair and current global impact chair of her sorority (Alpha Kappa Alpha), she has led projects such as an event turning t-shirts into resusable grocery bags, a vegan dinner to talk about sustainable food consumption, and a Soles for Souls campaign turning old shoes into water that can be transported to water-deprived communities.

Taylor will soon begin a co-op with LG&E, working in plant operations and gas storage, but noted that as she continues developing herself for a career in chemical engineering, she will carry her experiences on the Minds in Motion Project with her.

“It reassured me that I like doing research outside of my own major and outside of a lab,” Taylor said.

Ann Hagan-Grigsby’s path to Park DuValle Community Health Center was an unexpected one. Growing up as a black female in Washington D. C., Hagan-Grisby worked hard from a young age, earning a spot as a National Merit Semi-Finalist in high school. She studied hard but did not think she would be able to go to college. Instead, she decided to follow in her mother’s footsteps and go into the real estate business that her mother had successfully started. However, one of Hagan-Grigsby’s high school teachers and counselors saw so much potential in her that she helped her secure a full-ride scholarship to George Washington University.

But Hagan-Grigsby was still faced with doubt from those around her. White students made it clear that black students were not welcome at GW, and professors had little faith that she could become a doctor, instead telling her that she should study something easier because “medicine is not for people like you.” Even still, Hagan-Grigsby ended up at the University of Louisville, with a full ride to medical school. Hagan-Grigsby ultimately decided to choose a different path, which led her to Park DuValle Community Health Center.

In 1992, Hagan-Grigsby was working as a Medical Research Technician for the University of Louisville Kidney Disease Program. Someone remembered her from her time at the University of Louisville’s School of Medicine and invited her to apply for a position at the West Louisville Area Health Education Center, which required Hagan-Grigsby to work at Park DuValle. Park DuValle immediately felt like home to Hagan-Grigsby, who loved the ethnically diverse, family-like work environment located in West Louisville.

About seven months after she began her new job at Park DuValle, tragedy struck. Hagan-Grigsby’s house in the Portland neighborhood was firebombed, along with three others in her neighborhood. Thankfully, Hagan-Grigsby and her children made it out of the house unharmed, and it was through this tragedy that Hagan-Grigsby found support at Park DuValle. The staff—some of

whom Hagan-Grigsby barely knew—came together to help her and her family by collecting household items, clothing, and money. Hagan-Grigsby was touched by the staff who rallied around her as if they had known her for her whole life.

Since then, Hagan-Grigsby has become the CEO of Park DuValle, and she continues to provide support to the West Louisville community. In recent years, Park DuValle and Hagan-Grigsby have partnered with the Consortium to document how West Louisville residents experience well-being.

Hagan-Grigsby says that the research she has done with the Consortium is unique in that typically researchers come in with preconceived and oftentimes inaccurate assumptions about the people who live in West Louisville. Conversely, Dr. Meera Alagaraja and her team have focused on the aspects of well-being and the joy that the patients experience.

Working with the Consortium, Hagan-Grigsby has been able to truly collaborate with the researchers in an environment where the input of the staff and patients was highly valued. The purpose of the project “Elucidating the Stories of Wellbeing in the West Louisville Community: A Phenomenological Study” is to aid the clinic in the assessment of wellbeing and coping strategies for its patients.

When Dion Copeland started as a freshman at UofL, he noticed something was off.

“Just coming to campus, I was exposed to a lot of systematic oppression,” he said. For example, he noticed there weren’t a lot of people of color in key leadership positions in the university. “I’m just interested in seeing what keeps people from pursuing these roles. I want a greater understanding of why that happens, not only on campus, but around the United States.”

These and other racial justice-related interests keep Copeland—a sophomore from Lexington—busy filling many leadership roles while pursuing a secondary education degree at UofL. They have also drawn him to becoming one of the Consortium’s first undergraduate research fellows, a role in which he has worked on research related to HIV stigma and housing justice.

“Being able to sit on research teams that contribute to the advancement of marginalized people appealed to me,” he said. “It will give me that experience of learning how to find ground-breaking tools and methods to change things we don’t like.”

In addition to sitting on various university committees, a few of Copeland’s leadership positions include: Campus Ambassador, Freshman Orientation lead, Black Student Union president, College of Education and Human Development student president, Police Advisory Board member, and Chair of the African American Programming Fund, which provides funding for multicultural Resident Student Organizations.

Leadership roles consume all his free time, and Copeland has his sights set on a different leadership role: school principal.

By becoming a school principal, Copeland hopes to combat the school-to-prison pipeline (and other ways school systems fail children of color).

“I want to show kids that education is key. If they’re starting out just not being able to find that connection to school, they may never find it,” Copeland said. “I’d like to go into administration and connect people and foster those relationships.”

Brandeis Law student Samantha Wright is a single mom whose interests in law and community activism are driven by her children. As the Consortium’s law student fellow, Wright has pursued these interests as a research assistant with Kentucky Youth Advocates.

“As a mom, every decision I make includes my kids. It’s hard when you are researching school shootings, School Resource Officers, and lunch shaming practices to not feel emotionally compelled to do deliver research in line with my concerns and opinions as a mom,” Wright said. “KYA allowed me to research matters that I was unaware of as both a student and a mom.”

Last year, Wright spoke with a friend who worked for the Consortium and knew that she wanted to become involved. Almost immediately after her receipt of the Consortium’s one-year law fellowship, the Consortium staff connected her to Kentucky Youth Advocates (KYA). KYA had expressed a need for legal research support, and Wright’s background and interests were a perfect fit.

During the 2017-2018 academic year, Wright’s work with the KYA has involved various research matters including kinship and the D.O. v. Glisson case, Kentucky’s foster care program, new hearsay exceptions, and protections for victims under Marsy’s Law. She has also examined the visitation experiences of children of incarcerated parents and the experiences of LGBTQI juveniles in detention centers.

As for her next steps, Wright said that she will work to identify other community options for foster kids who have aged-out of care. She will also be working on a diversity committee at Brandeis.

Wright indicated that her time with KYA has helped her grow professionally and stay committed to her personal values.

“Doing this type of work allows for my professional and personal life to intersect with one another,” Wright said. “As a mom and a student, I often have to engage in these roles separately, and they seldom cross into one another. However, this combination motivates me. I am able to be both an advocate and a mom, and the balance of the two became a lot easier because I could relate and I wanted to know what was being decided and how it would affect my boys. I have both advocacy and policy experience with law, and KYA allowed more experiences and research aligned with my personal needs and values.”

Modern Languages professor Dr. Lluís Baixauli-Olmos had social justice research ideas boiling for months when, by chance, he met Dr. Katy Hopkins, a professor in Counseling and Human Development, at a neighborhood chili cook-off.

Baixauli-Olmos was planning a graduate course on “Interpreting in Social Services and in Educational Settings,” seeking to infuse social justice aims into the course. When he heard about Dr. Hopkins’s work as Director of the Cardinal Success Program—which seeks to increase access to quality counseling and psychology services for residents from traditionally underserved neighborhoods in West Louisville—he thought, “This is it.”

Together, Baixauli-Olmos and Hopkins formed “The TIMIS Project: Trauma-Informed Mental Health Interpreting Services for Victims of Domestic Violence.” This project partners with the Cardinal Success Program and the Center for Women and Families to provide interpretation services in order for Spanish-speaking clients to be able to receive mental health care.

“The core of this project is to counter structural inequalities by making it possible for women treated both in the Center for Women and Families and the Cardinal Success Program to receive language services,” he said. “In theory, this project would allow victims to recover some areas of their lives that maybe they have not been able to keep up with because of their traumatic experiences.”

The project is in its early stages. Team members are meeting with partners, identifying language needs, and establishing a structure for the free interpreting services. In the future, Baixauli-Olmos hopes this project will guide other local offices that work with victims of trauma who may also be linguistic minorities.

Doing this work fills a necessary research gap—there’s not much scholarship on interpreting services for domestic violence survivors in mental health setting—and it also fills a personal motivation for Baixauli-Olmos—using his PhD to forward social justice aims.

“What motivates me... is facilitating the provision of a necessary and crucial service to people who cannot receive basic services because of language barriers—services needed for those people to recover control, to go back to having a more healthy life,“ he said.

As a Middle and Secondary Education major, Kaleb Clemons has observed a lot of students, but one that stands out to him is the one who always fell asleep—but not for the reasons you might imagine.

Kaleb recalls how day after day, the student fell asleep at his desk. His teacher would wake him up and try to engage him in classroom activities, but the student would fall right back asleep.

Rather than simply labeling this student as “bad,” or “lazy,” Kaleb and the teacher he was observing had a long conversation with the student, in which they discovered that he was working—often very late into the night—in order to support his whole family.

This taught Kaleb an important lesson: “Teachers must get to know their students and learn about their lives so that we understand why students act the way they do. The student in the story was far from lazy, and he wasn’t even a bad kid; he just had priorities that at the moment are more important than paying attention in class.”

Kaleb has continued to explore how to support students like this through participating in a Consortium-funded research project—“Transforming Learning Communities: A Multi-Year Project Supporting Teachers of Adolescents.” This project leverages student perspectives, consciousness building, and ongoing self-reflection and action to support long-term trauma-informed teacher education around classroom management.

Through this project, Kaleb has learned classroom management strategies to help him more effectively support struggling students. Acknowledging trauma’s prevalence among K-12 classrooms, the project engages pre-service teachers in conversations about structural issues such as marginalization, racism, community violence, and experiences of people who are undocumented, as well as non-judgmental, trauma-sensitive teaching dispositions and practices.

The importance of social justice in academic research is nothing foreign to Dr. Lesley Harris of UofL’s Kent School of Social Work. Dr. Harris attributes her passion for social justice work to her experience of living in various communities across America,and learning to understand the root causes of the social problems that affect those communities, as well as their solutions. She notes that, even from the very beginning of her journey in higher education, the literature she encountered, as well as her practicum experience, allowed her to see the intrinsic relationship between social justice and the field of social work.

Dr. Harris is the Primary Investigator on the Consortium-supported “Our World, Our Say” research project, a community based participatory action research project which utilizes the Photovoice methodology. Photovoice is a qualitative method that allows community participants to utilize photography to document and reflect their reality. This method is typically used to shed light on the unique perspective of a given community and on their perceived needs and strengths, and findings can often be used to advocate for social change. Dr. Marion Hambrick (CEHD) and Dr. Kyoungmee Byun (Fine Arts) work alongside Dr. Harris and their community partner –HIV and Health Care Support Center located in Vietnam— to bring this project to fruition. Together, with the help of youth in Hai Phong, the project hopes to raise awareness about the issues facing these young people through photography exhibits and other forms of advocacy.

“Our World, Our Say” started this past summer in Hai Phong, Vietnam, where researchers worked with 25 young people who are being raised by their grandparents because their parents passed away due to HIV/AIDS-related illnesses. Using photography and other methods, the youth involved in this project identified HIV and reproductive health, child labor, child sexual abuse, and substance use as the most important issues to focus on in their community.

The trandisciplinarity of the “Our World, Our Say” team is one of its greatest strengths. When asked about the benefits of working on a transdisciplinary team, Dr. Harris expressed gratitude to her co-researchers and the expertise they each bring:

“We wanted to be innovative in our methodology, [Dr. Hambrick] is an expert in social network analysis[...]. Very few Photovoice projects involve researchers who are artists, but to have an artist[Dr. Byun]has been invaluable, she’s given insight into how to exhibit the space to increase the message.”

To aspiring social justice researchers and activists, Dr. Harris recommends the workbook Me and White Supremacy, by Layla Saad–but she notes that it is specifically aimed at well-meaning white people. The workbook provides guided self-work and self-reflection about how individuals may be upholding the systems of white supremacy. Dr. Harris promises, “This[book]has completely, and overwhelming changed my life!”
